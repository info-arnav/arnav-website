I graduated recently.

It was a unique day, partly because it did not feel like a clean ending. I came
to college as one kind of person and left as someone noticeably different. The
degree was the visible thing everyone could point to, but it was never the
whole story for me.

When I first came to college, I thought I already knew most of what mattered. I
used to wonder why a formal degree was even necessary. In a very narrow sense,
that thought was not completely wrong. The degree did feel formal. I do not
think college changed me because of what I learned academically in classrooms.

But my life changed anyway.

College changed my personality. It put me around people from backgrounds,
families, cities, languages, habits, and ways of thinking that were different
from mine. Slowly, almost without noticing it, I started seeing the world with
more context. My perspective became less rigid. I became more aware of people,
of ambition, of insecurity, of kindness, of how different everyone's journey
can be even when we are all standing in the same campus.

That is probably the part I will remember most.

I met some great people. I made great friends. And for some reason, unlike
school, I do not know how long all of them will stay close. College friendships
feel different. They are intense because you grow up around each other, but
after graduation everyone starts moving in different directions very quickly.

I hope I never lose them.

If college taught me anything clearly, it is that the most important thing you
build is relationships. Not just contacts, not just a network, but real
relationships. People who show up. People who make ordinary days memorable.
People who quietly change the way you speak, think, work, and carry yourself.

You owe a lot of your personality to the people who were around you while you
were becoming yourself.

Steve Jobs once said that most people never ask for help, and that has stayed
with me. College made me understand it in a very real way. People are usually
more willing to help than we assume. Sometimes the only thing missing is the
courage to ask.

I saw that everywhere on campus. With friends, with teachers, with seniors,
with juniors, and even with people we usually do not think about enough. The
security guards, the staff, the people who quietly keep the place running.
Sometimes just being kind to them changed everything. A simple good morning, a
small conversation, or treating them with basic respect meant more than it
looked like from the outside.

The world is a unique place that way. People remember how you make them feel.
If you are kind to someone, they often go far beyond what they are required to
do. Not because they owe you anything, but because kindness makes people want
to show up for you too.

That is why being there for friends matters. Help should not always feel like a
transaction or a favor. Sometimes it is just a way of paying back the invisible
debt you owe to the people who shaped you. If someone gave you confidence,
company, perspective, or even just a reason to keep showing up, then being
there for them later is the least you can do.

So yes, I graduated with a degree. But I also graduated with memories, a changed
personality, a better perspective, and people I hope remain part of my life for
a long time.

Great journey. Great college. Great life.
