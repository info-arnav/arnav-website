Today, April 7, 2026, I’m launching [Vox](https://www.vox-ai.chat/) on Product Hunt.

I’ve built a lot of things before this.
Tools, experiments, side projects, half-finished ideas, and weird systems that were fun to make but easy to leave behind.

So Vox is not the first thing I’ve built.
But it is one of the first things I’m trying to put out properly and stand behind in public.

## AI is already normal

One thing that keeps sticking with me is this:
AI itself does not feel futuristic anymore.

My friends already talk to ChatGPT and Claude like it is normal, because it is.
Talking to bots is not the surprising part now.

What still feels weirdly technical is everything around it.

The setup.
The accounts.
The API keys.
The model choices.
The providers.
The permissions.
The whole feeling that before you can use something useful, you first have to understand how the plumbing works.

That is the part I keep coming back to with Vox.

## Setup is still the broken part

A lot of AI products are technically impressive, but they still make normal users feel like they are configuring infrastructure.

And local AI has this problem even more.

A lot of the space is still built in a way that makes people feel like they need to understand what model they are running, what settings they should pick, what hardware they need, and what tradeoff they are making before they even get to the product itself.

I do not think that is where this should go.

If AI is going to become truly personal, then the setup has to get out of the way.
It cannot feel like a science project.

That is a big part of what I want Vox to be:
download it,
give it access to the things it needs,
and talk.

No ceremony.
No maze.
One obvious path.

## Why I care about stronger personal machines

The deeper reason I believe in this direction is that I do not think today’s setup-heavy AI world is the final form.

Right now, a lot of AI is shaped by the limits of the moment:
expensive inference,
model constraints,
cloud dependence,
and products that still feel rented from a website instead of integrated into your own computer.

But that is going to change.

Machines are getting stronger.
Models are getting better.
Inference is getting cheaper.

You can already start to feel this on stronger personal hardware.
On Apple Silicon machines.
On more powerful desktops.
On systems like a Mac Studio today.

Over time, more of this capability is going to feel normal on everyday personal computers.

And when that happens, I think more useful AI should move closer to the user.
Closer to your files.
Closer to your context.
Closer to your own machine.

Not because local is a nice marketing word, but because it gives you something important:
privacy,
lower friction,
better context,
and software that feels more personal.

## The vision behind Vox

The future of AI is not just smarter models in bigger data centers.

It is stronger personal machines running more useful AI closer to the user, with more privacy, less setup, and tighter integration with the computer itself.

That is the vision I care about.

I want AI to feel less like a website and more like a real layer of your computer.
I want it to act, not just answer.
I want it to understand your context over time.
And I want it to be simple enough that normal people can use it without feeling like they are installing infrastructure.

That is what I’m trying to build with Vox.

Not another chatbot.
Not another AI tab.
Something closer to a real computer assistant.

## Why Vox looks the way it does

That is why the product is voice-first.
That is why I care about it working closer to the operating system.
That is why privacy matters.
That is why I keep coming back to zero setup.

If this future is real, the winning product will not be the one with the most knobs.
It will be the one that hides the complexity best.

Power should increase.
Friction should disappear.

That is the bar.

## Why I’m launching now

Shipping publicly is different from building privately.

When something is just sitting on your laptop, it is easy to believe in it.
When you launch it, people tell you very quickly whether they actually get it, whether they trust it, and whether the thing solves a real problem or just sounds interesting.

That is what I want from this launch.

Not fake hype.
Not vanity.
Signal.

I want to know whether this idea resonates:
that the future of AI should feel personal, private, and simple, and that powerful AI on your own machine should stop feeling like tech for technical people.

## If you check out Vox today

If you try Vox, tell me what you actually think.

Tell me what feels useful.
Tell me what still feels confusing.
Tell me where it feels like the future, and where it still feels too technical.

I’ve built a lot of things before.
This is one I want to keep pushing.

I hope it works.
